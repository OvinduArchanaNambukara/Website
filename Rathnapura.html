Ratnapura

A bustling, congested city, Ratnapura hosts few travellers but is a famous trading centre for the area’s wealth of gemstones. As you explore the town, you're sure to encounter some hustle from pushy salespeople eager to flog you a gemstone (or a piece of polished glass). Another reason to visit Ratnapura is that it’s the take-off point for one of the oldest routes up Adam’s Peak.

Udawalawe National Park

Udawalawe national park, located within 200 km to the south east of the commercial capital of Colombo, Sri Lanka is an elegantly a wonderful eco tourism park. The park expands over 30821 heck and has earned a reputation for its wild elephants that surpass over the area (there are about 500 in the park in herds of up to 100). The national park nests over the Sabaragamuwa district, Monaragala district and also Uva province. Additionally, the park aims to conserve the variety of flora and fauna ensuring the water catchment areas reserved for animal kind. The Udawalawe national park is lovely home for wild buffaloes, mongooses, bandicoots, foxes, water monitor lizards, crocodiles, The Rusty-spotted cat, fishing cat. Sri Lankan leopard is permanent members of the Fieldale family present in Udawalawe. The Sri Lankan sloth bear is seldom seen because of its rarity. Sri Lankan sambar deer, Sri Lankan axis deer, Indian muntjac, Sri Lankan spotted chevrotain, wild boar sloth bears, and the occasional leopard. Thus you can involve in bird watching too. Being located and bordered with wet and dry zone areas, the plains dominate the topography. It is recorded that some endemic floral species like Hopea cordifolia, Memecylon petrolatum, Erythroxylon zeylanicum, Jasminum Angustifolia are found under the canopy. Panicum maximum and Imperata cylindric which are concerned as favorites of the wild elephants are common in the park. This has been a reason to accommodate such a large number of herds of elephants in the national park. You will be able to see oriental garden lizards, mugger crocodiles, water monitors and also painted lip lizards around the area. If you are touring with a helpful guide, try to identify the species of snakes that you may see on your journey with him.

Sinharaja Forest Reserve

The Sinharaja forest reserve is a tropical rain forest in the south western part of Sri Lanka and covers an area of 11187 ha. The reserve is bordered by Galle, Matara, and Ratnapura districts. It is reckoned as a main eco tourism attraction and an evergreen forest in Sri Lanka. The forest is ideal for bird watching, nature loving and exploring the nature. The Sinharaja forest reserve was commenced as a world biosphere reserved in 1978 and a World heritage site in 1978. Since it was identified for nontimber forest products, genetic resources and the high range of bio diversity, declared as a forest reserve in 1875 under the Waste Lands Ordinance.
